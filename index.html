The Evolution of Cultural Practices
from 1893 till 21st Century

Ganesh Chaturthi · Shivaji Jayanti · and the making of political weapons
essay on Western India · cultural history 2026 · critical inquiry

The purpose of this is to investigate the history of some cultural practices introduced, mainly in Western India and its immediate consequences since the inception. I seek to answer a rather debatable question: How has culture been used, as a weapon, over a century, to fulfil specific goals of times? The goal is to analyse the demographics of the two festivals—Ganesh Chaturthi and Shivaji Jayanti—and whether or not its significance is distorted and meaning lost in translation of politics.

Origins: revival, riots, and the making of a public festival

In order to understand the dynamics involved in the building and evolution of the mammoth festivities of Ganesh Chaturthi and Shivaji Jayanti, it would be helpful to look into the context and background against which the divergent interpretations developed. The period marked by violence, riot and religious feuds was brewing for revival of language politics in Maharashtra. The Congress Programme was made into a people's movement. The crowd was married to riots, be it Hindu or Muslim, and fears of an outbreak were prevalent for months. “A large concourse of Muhammedans issued from the Jama Masjid and with shouts of 'Din Din' commenced to attack a Hindu temple” (Menon 2010, 64). A Muslim mob had indulged in an orgy of violence in the course of which Hindu temples were destroyed. These riots had deep repercussions not only in peninsular Bombay, but also beyond. This had led to Hindus demanding, to the government, the prohibition of cow slaughter. Tension was surging all over Maharashtra. It was the time when Tilak was already a national celebrity. He had not yet revived the Ganpati festival which later did so much to inflame communal feelings, but his speeches had already taken on an anti‑Muslim tone and the tension between the two communities was growing (Menon 2010, 64).

Tilak's pluralistic, multireligious, multilingualistic and multi‑ethnic ideologies provided the manifesto for the democratic procession. The aim was to create a nation (India) which would not only be united in a sense to fight British Colonial Rule, but also a secular and democratic country which would preserve the interest of the majority citizens. What Tilak lacked in his 'Right' approach was the inclusion of Muslims who were also evenly populated then.

He established this spirited enthusiasm in the masses by glorifying the culture of Ganesh Chaturthi. This was a successful political intervention attested to by the history of the 20th century where all mass religious denominations have changed their religious celebrations to the centre piece of their religious imagination and community identity (Economic and Political Weekly 2012, 7). Thus, Ganesh Chaturthi became a cultural symbol of Maharashtra, and what was considered to be holy during the Maratha Empire in the regions of Pune, was becoming a political ideology. By 1893 the festival had re‑emerged as a public event wherein all communities participated together. It healed, for at least some period, the political conundrum caused in Bombay and Pune involving cow protection. This new Ganpati festival was modelled as a mirror image of the Shia festival of Muharram.

However, an analytical study into the ideology of Lokmanya Tilak and Nationalism makes a proper distinction between Hinduism and Hindutva, since Tilak focused on Hinduism but he was never the supporter of Hindutva (Bhosale, 2009).

Tilak, in his newspaper Kesari, wrote: “A pan‑Hindu identity is sought to be forged; the importance of the Maratha‑Brahmin unity for economic prosperity comes to be underlined and caste oppression is displaced as 'mutual hatred and jealousies that can be overcome for the sake of pride in one's religion'” (Rege 2000, 201). The re‑invention of Hinduism and lack of interest in Hindutva needs to be underlined here.

Shivaji Jayanti, Phule and the Satyashodhak counter‑tradition

Ganesh Chaturthi as a public gathering gave rise not only to a certain notion of sub‑national identity, but also commenced a convenient vehicle for creating communal divisions as well as reasserting caste and gender lines. The public tradition or, in that particular sense, communal (sarvajanik) religious festivals was a conscious effort by Tilak to unite the community for the nation (Economic and Political Weekly 2012, 7). Ganesh Mela in its emergence drew upon the popularity of the Satyashodhak Jalsas. The Prarthana Samaj and the Satyashodhak Samaj were some of the chief organs of reform in Maharashtra and the influence of the ideals adopted by these societies was evident in the versions of Maratha history and Shivaji Jayanti (Vartak 1999, 1127). Shivaji Jayanti was popularised by Ranade and Phule who were also founders of these organisations.

The content of the Satyashodhak Tamashas or Jalsas was drawn from the ballads, songs, abhangs and poems of the Satyashodhak leaders. By the 1910s, there were at least 29 Satyashodhak Jalsas in western Maharashtra. The organisers and performers came from lower and middle castes (Marathas, Sonar, Nhavi, Mahar and Mangs) and there seems to have been a rich textual give and take between the different Jalsa troupes. In Pune, at least up till 1920, the major base of the Jalsas was drawn from the Mali (gardener), Shimpi (tailor), Khatik (meat slaughterer) and Dhangar (shepherd) castes (Rege 2000, 198). The Jalsas with their explicit critique of Brahminism had emerged as a significant mode of claiming equality in the public sphere. Phule drew upon a pre‑Aryan, Kshatriya tradition with which he confronted orthodox Hinduism. For Phule, the identity was that of a Kshatriya origin, and Maratha ruler Shivaji as an assertion (O'Hanlon 1985, 172). Phule imagined that the povada had a specific social and political message, and that he, a man of the masses, was interpreting Shivaji for them (O'Hanlon 1985, 170). Exhibiting a well‑developed understanding of the region and its culture, Phule was able to effectively combine the imagery of the western Indian landscape with the heroic deeds of Shivaji to give the povada the necessary emotive force (Vartak 1999, 1129). Phule's ability to represent resistance made the image naturally relevant in the context of colonialism. From then on, the religious festivals were transformed into a huge public celebration, which also led a pathway to sharpened religious differences and to communal violence.

Media, masses and divergent trajectories

It would also be helpful to look at the media used by these leaders. Both Phule and Tilak used the more populist means of spreading their views. Phule through his use of the traditional folk medium of the povada and Tilak through the use of the Ganesh festival which was backed by the print media, i.e., through his articles in the Kesari and the Mahratha. Tilak's approach, according to me, was far more flamboyant, an approach that appealed to masses in growing industrialization and globalization in Mumbai. On the contrary, Phule's method was restricted to the rural and lower‑class Maharashtrians.

The Ganesh Melas and Jayanti, in fact, became popular ways of naturalising the divide of social versus political for it was grounded on the assumption that difference and conflict between castes were only cultural/social and not political. A documentation, both historical and contemporary, of such regional and caste‑based cultural practices suggests that there has been a marginalisation of these practices by bourgeois forms of art and entertainment; it may be argued that popular practices are neither just traditions of resistance nor just forms on which the bourgeois forms are superimposed. To understand the popular cultural forms in history only in terms of 'folk' and as contrasting with formalism and the contemporary popular, which is understood already and always as only 'mass‑mediated'—is to commit both a historical and political distortion (Rege 2000, 197). It is true that Shivaji Jayanti occupied a position of pre‑eminence in Marathi folk culture. The strong and already existing emotional identification with the glories of the Maratha past enabled politicians and ideologues across the political spectrum, from Phule to Ranade to Tilak to Savarkar, to employ the figure of Shivaji to win over different social groups.

From cultural symbol to right‑wing machinery: Shiv Sena and the 20th century turn

Today, the public religious festivals have established themselves in the social fabric of the state. It must be cogent to say that these cultural signatures provide the politicians and marketeers with captive collectives of population with similar language, perspective and caste. This has been the result of deliberate effort—almost all of it of right‑wing and revolutionary political forces. The Hindutva forces have underlined the continued existence of the festival as an assertion of an unchanging Indian reality amidst a world that is ceaselessly changing. In late 1960s the formation of Marathi‑speaking state inaugurated the era of ideology of Maratha domination. The upper caste and class people took the initiative for leadership for the formation of the different state, the state which would be governed by the Marathi‑speaking people and not anyone else. (The nature of such a political idea, in a metropolitan city, still chafes me.) It led us to the formation of the historical party, Shiv Sena.

Shiv Sena was formed in 1966. It was a platform, led by Balasaheb Thackeray, to protest for the interests of the so‑called natives in the financial capital of India, Bombay. Shiv Sena used these public gatherings to destroy the strong trade‑union movement. While communal violence, associated with literary or fundamentalist understandings of religious narrative, was an all‑too‑common feature of the modern world and not confined to Bombay or India, the belligerent nature of conservative Hindutva movements, such as the Shiv Sena (a stark contrast to Tilak's imagination) led to a beginning of an era of linguistic politics in the modern India.

Shivaji Jayanti, which was once celebrated in Maharashtra as a symbol of resistance and pride, is now recognised as a cultural territory of the Shiv Sena, a Maharashtra chauvinism and a resurgent sub‑nationalism. Shiv Sena targets upper caste, middle and lower middle, and not the Dalit section which was the crux of Phule's ideology. These festivals are occasions for building up the Shiv Sena ethos. Even in areas which are not preponderantly Maharashtrian, like Colaba, on Ganapati day hordes of boys walk on the streets with Shiv Sena vests, and the saffron flag is seen everywhere. The entire Shiv Sena machinery is activated during these festivals.

The events since 1893 raise more questions than I have answers for. Why did a city which welcomed people of every race and religion move from a culture of tolerance to enforcing bigotry? Why do temples and mosques which have coexisted together for years suddenly plunge a city into chaos?

The riots that started in 1893 show clearly the tension created in the first instance. A line that is echoed even today. Many myths prevailed in those days as well. In fact with each riot, history shows that people began moving into places dominated by their own community for the sake of safety and security. The strong right‑wing movement from pre‑independence also contributed to much tension which has continued over the years even after 1947.
